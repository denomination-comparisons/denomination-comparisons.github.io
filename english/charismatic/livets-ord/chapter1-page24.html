Secondly, inspiration implied painstaking study of original sources. Luke, the evangelist, is an example of this. He says:

...it seemed good to me also, having had perfect understanding of all things from the very first, to write to you an orderly account, most excellent Theophilus, that you may know the certainty of those things in which you were instructed (Luke 1:3-4).

The Bible consists of carefully formulated teaching, recorded faithfully and in order so that its authenticity appears to all. Although Luke's approach was different from John's, both of them received God's Word by inspiration of the Holy Spirit.

Some people have denied the inspiration of the Word of God, arguing that only the general content is inspired, not every word. This assertion is wrong for several reasons:

1. Thoughts cannot be communicated without words.

2. The Bible often refers to individual words. For example, Paul bases one argument on the single word 'seed' in Galatians 3:16. Furthermore, he says, These things we also speak, not in words which man's wisdom teaches but which the Holy Spirit teaches, comparing spiritual things with spiritual (1 Corinthians 2:13). Even individual words are given by God. How could we otherwise know when single words are important or not?
